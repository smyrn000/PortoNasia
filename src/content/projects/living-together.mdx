## Living, together

*Living Together* began with a simple question: how does an interior make a group
of strangers feel, quickly and quietly, like they belong to the same place? The
project reorganises a communal residence around generosity — generous daylight,
generous shared rooms, and a generous sense that there is always somewhere to be
with others, and somewhere to be alone.

Rather than treating circulation as leftover space, the plan turns it into the
building's social spine. A long **red-screen corridor** runs the length of the
ground floor like an interior street, lined with pin-boards, ledges and built-in
benches that invite people to pause, lean and talk. Daylight filters through the
perforated screen, drawing a shifting pattern across the floor through the day.

## A language of warmth

The material palette is deliberately tight so the architecture reads as one idea.
**Birch plywood** lines the private rooms and joinery, lending warmth and a sense
of craft. A single **terracotta red** frames thresholds, screens and key moments,
acting as a wayfinding cue and a thread that ties the whole building together.
Soft accents — green, cobalt and a single, joyful yellow — appear where people
gather, keeping the communal rooms playful without ever feeling busy.

The communal heart is the **dining hall and kitchens**: long shared tables under
clusters of spherical pendants, an open kitchen built to cook together, and softer
lounge corners that spill off to the side. From here the building loosens into
quieter wings of private rooms.

## Designed for everyone

Accessibility is not an addition but a starting point. Bathrooms, thresholds and
furniture were designed for accessible use throughout, and a **modular furniture
system** lets each private room adapt to its resident — the same kit of parts,
arranged for different needs. Level access, clear sightlines and generous turning
space are built into the plan rather than retrofitted.

The result is an interior that is calm but never cold: a place engineered for
togetherness, where the architecture quietly does the work of bringing people
into the same warm, well-lit room.
